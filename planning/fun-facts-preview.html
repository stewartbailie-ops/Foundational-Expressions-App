Tax 45 facts
#1South Africa's tax year runs from 1 March to 28 February — not the calendar year.
#2You must submit a tax return if you earn more than R500,000 per year from a single employer.
#3The first R40,000 of capital gains per year is exempt from tax for individuals.
#4Interest income up to R23,800 (under 65) or R34,500 (65+) is tax-free per year.
#5Contributions to a retirement annuity are tax-deductible up to 27.5% of your taxable income.
#6South Africa uses a progressive tax system — the more you earn, the higher your marginal rate.
#7The top marginal income tax rate in South Africa is 45%.
#8Dividends received from South African companies are taxed at a flat 20% dividends withholding tax.
#9Estate duty is 20% on dutiable estates up to R30 million, and 25% on amounts above that.
#10The estate duty abatement is R3.5 million — estates below this threshold pay no estate duty.
#11A surviving spouse inherits the unused portion of their partner's R3.5 million estate duty abatement.
#12Donating more than R100,000 per year triggers donations tax at 20% — 25% above R30 million.
#13SARS can audit your tax returns for up to five years after submission.
#14Working from home? You may be able to claim a portion of rent, electricity, and internet as a tax deduction.
#15Capital Gains Tax is not a separate tax — gains are included in your income at a 40% inclusion rate for individuals.
#16The effective CGT rate for an individual at the top bracket is 18% — calculated as 40% × 45%.
#17Your primary residence CGT exclusion is R2 million — the first R2m of gain on your home is tax-free.
#18Medical aid contributions give a tax credit of R364/month for the main member and R246 for the first dependant.
#19Retirement annuity contributions made before 28 February can reduce your taxable income for that tax year.
#20SARS's eFiling portal lets you file returns, check refund status, and manage your tax affairs online.
#21If you are under 65 and earn below R95,750 per year, you pay no income tax.
#22South African tax bracket thresholds are adjusted every year in the February Budget Speech.
#23A tax directive from SARS is required before any lump sum from a retirement fund is paid out.
#24Severance packages above R500,000 are taxed — the first R500,000 is tax-free, once in a lifetime.
#25Provisional tax applies to anyone who earns income not subject to PAYE — such as rental or freelance income.
#26Provisional taxpayers must pay in August and February each year.
#27Underestimating your provisional tax by more than 20% can attract a penalty from SARS.
#28Travel allowances are only deductible for business kilometres — a logbook is required as proof.
#29SARS uses third-party data from banks, employers, and medical aids to pre-populate your tax return.
#30Living annuity income is fully taxable — it is not treated as a capital drawdown.
#31If you emigrate from South Africa, you may trigger an 'exit tax' on deemed disposal of your worldwide assets.
#32South Africa taxes on a residency basis — if you are a tax resident, your worldwide income is taxable.
#33The foreign income exemption of R1.25 million applies to qualifying employment income earned abroad for 183+ days.
#34You have three years to claim a tax refund from SARS before the claim lapses.
#35Tax-free savings accounts (TFSAs) allow you to invest up to R36,000 per year with zero tax on growth or withdrawals.
#36The lifetime TFSA contribution limit is R500,000 — exceeding it attracts a 40% penalty tax.
#37TFSA contributions cannot be replaced once withdrawn — you lose that contribution room permanently.
#38You can have more than one TFSA, but the annual and lifetime limits apply across all accounts combined.
#39South Africa has double taxation agreements with more than 80 countries to prevent being taxed twice on the same income.
#40SARS can issue a garnishee order on your salary if you have outstanding tax debt.
#41Penalty interest on late tax payments is charged at the prime rate plus a fixed percentage.
#42A tax clearance certificate — now called Compliance Status from SARS — is required for large transactions and tenders.
#43Wear and tear deductions are available on assets used in your business or trade.
#44Medical out-of-pocket expenses above four times the medical tax credit may be deductible if you are over 65.
#45Keeping detailed records of all income and deductions for at least five years is a legal requirement.
Retirement 45 facts
#46South Africa's two-pot retirement system came into effect on 1 September 2024 — changing how all retirement funds work.
#47Under the two-pot system, one-third of new contributions go into an accessible savings pot and two-thirds into a preserved retirement pot.
#48You can make one withdrawal per tax year from your savings pot — the minimum withdrawal is R2,000.
#49Withdrawals from the savings pot are taxed at your marginal income tax rate — they are not tax-free.
#50The retirement pot can only be accessed at retirement and must be used to purchase an annuity.
#51The vested pot holds all retirement savings accumulated before 1 September 2024 — the old rules apply to this portion.
#52A retirement annuity (RA) is one of the most tax-efficient savings vehicles available in South Africa.
#53RA funds are protected from creditors — they cannot be attached in insolvency proceedings.
#54You can start drawing from your retirement annuity from age 55, even if you are still working.
#55At retirement, you can take up to one-third of your RA as a lump sum — the remainder must purchase an annuity.
#56The first R550,000 of a retirement lump sum is tax-free — this is a once-in-a-lifetime benefit across all funds.
#57Withdrawing from a pension or provident fund before retirement triggers tax — often at a rate higher than waiting.
#58A living annuity lets you draw between 2.5% and 17.5% of your capital per year — you choose the drawdown rate.
#59A guaranteed life annuity pays a fixed income for life — you cannot outlive your money.
#60The average South African lives 20–30 years after retiring — investment returns in retirement are as important as before.
#61Only 6% of South Africans can afford to retire comfortably — financial planning makes a significant difference.
#62You need roughly 15–17 times your final annual salary saved to maintain your lifestyle in retirement.
#63Inflation erodes purchasing power — your retirement income must grow at least in line with CPI.
#64Starting to save at 25 instead of 35 can more than double your retirement outcome due to compound interest.
#65Emigration before age 55 may allow you to withdraw RA funds — but tax applies and advice is essential.
#66South Africa's SASSA old-age grant is around R2,180/month — not sufficient to sustain most retirement lifestyles.
#67Preservation funds allow you to park your pension or provident fund when changing jobs without cashing out.
#68Employer contributions to a retirement fund are a valuable benefit — often undervalued in salary negotiations.
#69On death before retirement, your fund pays out to your nominated beneficiaries or dependants — not your estate.
#70The retirement fund trustee decides how to distribute death benefits — nominations guide but do not bind the trustees.
#71Keep your retirement fund beneficiary nominations updated after every major life event.
#72A retirement annuity has no set maturity date — you choose when to retire, from age 55 onwards.
#73The fees on a retirement product can erode up to 30% of your total returns over 30 years — always check the TER.
#74A total expense ratio (TER) below 1% per year is generally considered reasonable for a retirement fund.
#75Regulation 28 limits how much of your retirement fund can be invested in equities (75%) and offshore (45%).
#76Offshore exposure in retirement funds provides rand-hedging and access to global growth.
#77Group retirement schemes offered by employers often have lower fees than individual products.
#78Divorce can affect your retirement fund — a court order can award a portion to your ex-spouse.
#79A clean-break divorce order allows immediate payment from a retirement fund to the non-member spouse.
#80Disability before retirement can be financially devastating without income protection insurance in place.
#81You can switch living annuity providers without triggering tax — the fund transfers in-specie.
#82Joint life annuities cover both spouses — the survivor continues to receive income after the first death.
#83Inflation-linked annuities increase income annually in line with CPI — important for long retirements.
#84A guaranteed period on a life annuity ensures payments continue to your estate even if you die early.
#85Annuity rates depend on your age, gender, health, and interest rates at retirement — shop around before committing.
#86Phased retirement — gradually reducing hours before fully stopping — is increasingly common and financially beneficial.
#87Dread disease cover pays a lump sum on diagnosis of a serious illness — it is separate from disability cover.
#88Regular reviews of your retirement plan are essential — at least once a year with your financial advisor.
#89Balanced funds are commonly used in retirement portfolios, spreading risk across equities, bonds, and cash.
#90Phased drawdown strategies in retirement — reducing withdrawals in poor market years — help your capital last longer.
Wills & Estate 40 facts
#91More than 70% of South Africans die without a valid will — known as dying intestate.
#92When you die intestate, your assets are distributed according to the Intestate Succession Act — not your wishes.
#93Under intestate succession, a surviving spouse and children share the estate equally.
#94A will must be signed in front of two witnesses who are not beneficiaries and are over 14 years old.
#95Witnesses do not need to know the contents of your will — they only need to witness your signature.
#96Your will can be changed or revoked at any time while you are alive and mentally competent.
#97Getting married automatically revokes your existing will in South Africa — make a new one after marriage.
#98A will made before a divorce remains valid in South Africa — update it immediately after any divorce.
#99The executor of your estate administers and distributes your assets — choose someone trustworthy and capable.
#100An executor is entitled to a fee of 3.5% (plus VAT) of the gross value of your estate.
#101Your estate can take 12–24 months to wind up — beneficiaries may have no access to funds during this period.
#102Liquid assets — cash and life policies — can provide immediate funds to dependants while the estate is wound up.
#103Life insurance paid to a named beneficiary bypasses the estate entirely — it is not subject to estate duty.
#104Funeral policies are separate from life insurance — they provide quick cash for burial costs, often within 48 hours.
#105A testamentary trust in your will protects assets for minor children or vulnerable beneficiaries.
#106Minor children cannot inherit directly — a guardian's fund or testamentary trust must hold assets until they reach majority.
#107The Office of the Master of the High Court oversees estate administration in South Africa.
#108A living will records your medical wishes if you become incapacitated — it is separate from your last will.
#109An enduring power of attorney allows someone to manage your affairs if you become mentally incapacitated.
#110Digital assets — crypto, online accounts, intellectual property — should be documented and included in estate planning.
#111Immovable property in another country is governed by that country's succession laws, not South Africa's.
#112Business interests should be addressed in your will — a buy-and-sell agreement protects co-owners on death.
#113A buy-and-sell agreement funds the surviving business partner to buy out the deceased's share.
#114Retirement funds and living annuities do NOT form part of your deceased estate — they pass directly to beneficiaries.
#115Keeping your estate liquid is critical — illiquid estates force the sale of assets to pay debts and taxes.
#116Estate planning is not only for the wealthy — everyone with dependants needs a valid will.
#117Charitable bequests in your will reduce the dutiable value of your estate for estate duty purposes.
#118A spousal bequest rollover defers estate duty until the surviving spouse passes away.
#119Marriage in community of property means your estates are merged — all assets and debts are shared equally.
#120Marriage out of community of property with accrual entitles each spouse to half the other's estate growth.
#121An antenuptial contract (ANC) defines your matrimonial property regime — it must be registered before marriage.
#122An ANC must be executed by a notary before the wedding — it cannot be backdated after marriage.
#123Family trusts can protect assets from creditors and manage inter-generational wealth transfer.
#124Section 4(q) of the Estate Duty Act: bequests to a surviving spouse are fully deductible for estate duty.
#125Review your will every three to five years — or immediately after any major life event.
#126Many South Africans discover after a loved one's death that their will was not correctly witnessed — making it invalid.
#127A lost will can cause significant delays and legal costs — keep the original in a safe, known location.
#128Telling your executor and a trusted family member where your will is kept is as important as making the will itself.
#129Multiple funeral policies are common in South Africa — consolidating can save money and simplify the claims process.
#130The cost of making a will is a fraction of the legal fees your estate will incur if you die without one.
Investment 40 facts
#131The Johannesburg Stock Exchange (JSE) is Africa's largest stock exchange by market capitalisation.
#132ETFs (Exchange-Traded Funds) offer low-cost, diversified market exposure — ideal for long-term passive investors.
#133The JSE All Share Index has returned approximately 12–14% per year on average over the last two decades.
#134Diversification reduces risk — spread investments across asset classes, geographies, and sectors.
#135Time in the market beats timing the market — consistent investing outperforms trying to predict highs and lows.
#136Rand-cost averaging — investing a fixed amount regularly — reduces the impact of market volatility on your portfolio.
#137Unit trusts pool money from multiple investors to buy a professionally managed portfolio.
#138Most active fund managers underperform their benchmark index over a 10-year period after fees.
#139The MSCI World Index tracks large and mid-cap companies across 23 developed markets — a benchmark for global exposure.
#140Inflation is the silent thief — an investment returning less than inflation loses real value every year.
#141South Africa's long-term inflation average is around 5–6% per year — your investments must consistently beat this.
#142A real return is your investment return minus inflation — this is what actually grows your wealth.
#143The Rule of 72: divide 72 by your annual return to find how many years it takes to double your money. At 8%, that's 9 years.
#144Compound interest grows your money exponentially — reinvesting your returns is the key to long-term wealth.
#145Risk and return are always related — higher potential returns come with higher potential for loss.
#146Your investment time horizon determines how much risk you can afford to take.
#147Equities outperform all other asset classes over the long run — but are highly volatile in the short term.
#148Bonds are loans to governments or companies — they pay regular interest and carry lower risk than equities.
#149REITs (Real Estate Investment Trusts) allow you to invest in property through the stock market with full liquidity.
#150Gold has historically been a safe-haven asset during economic uncertainty and periods of rand weakness.
#151Offshore investing protects against rand depreciation and gives access to global growth opportunities.
#152South African residents can invest up to R11 million offshore per year via a tax clearance from SARS.
#153An additional R1 million per year can be invested offshore without a tax clearance — the single discretionary allowance.
#154A 3–6 month emergency fund in a liquid, interest-bearing account is the foundation of any financial plan.
#155Money market funds offer better returns than a cheque account and remain highly liquid — ideal for emergency funds.
#156Never invest money you cannot afford to lose in high-risk assets — protect your emergency fund first.
#157The Sharpe ratio measures return per unit of risk — a higher Sharpe ratio indicates better risk-adjusted performance.
#158Past performance does not guarantee future results — this is a legal disclosure for a very good reason.
#159Financial advisors in South Africa are legally required to act in your best interest under the FAIS Act.
#160A fee-based advisor charges for advice directly — a commission-based advisor earns from the products they sell.
#161Always read the minimum disclosure document (MDD) before investing in a unit trust or ETF.
#162ESG investing (Environmental, Social, Governance) allows you to align your portfolio with your values.
#163A balanced fund typically holds 60% equities and 40% bonds and cash — lower volatility than a pure equity fund.
#164Never concentrate all your investment in a single company — not even one you work for or believe in strongly.
#165Dollar-cost averaging into global ETFs is one of the most proven long-term wealth-building strategies available.
#166Investing in your children's education is one of the highest-returning investments you will ever make.
#167Liquidity means how quickly an asset can be converted to cash — property is illiquid, equities are liquid.
#168Section 12B tax deductions are available for qualifying renewable energy investments — ask your advisor.
#169The JSE has circuit breakers that halt trading if prices move too quickly — protecting investors from flash crashes.
#170Reviewing your investment portfolio at least annually ensures your asset allocation still matches your goals.
Budgeting & Saving 40 facts
#171The 50/30/20 rule: 50% of income on needs, 30% on wants, and 20% on savings and debt repayment.
#172Paying yourself first — saving before spending — is the single most effective saving habit you can build.
#173An emergency fund should be in a separate account so you are not tempted to dip into it for everyday spending.
#174South Africa's household savings rate is among the lowest in the world — most South Africans spend more than they save.
#175Credit card debt is among the most expensive forms of borrowing — interest rates can exceed 20% per year.
#176Paying only the minimum amount on a credit card can take years to clear even a small balance.
#177Store credit and retail accounts often carry the highest interest rates — use them sparingly and always pay in full.
#178A budget is not a punishment — it is a plan that tells your money where to go before you spend it.
#179Zero-based budgeting allocates every rand of income to a specific purpose — nothing is left unaccounted for.
#180Tracking your spending for just one month reveals habits and patterns you did not know you had.
#181Subscriptions add up fast — audit yours every six months and cancel anything you no longer use.
#182Meal planning and buying in bulk can reduce your monthly grocery bill by 20–30%.
#183Financing a car over 72 months can cost you almost double the original purchase price in total.
#184A new car is not an asset — it depreciates the moment it leaves the showroom floor.
#185Avoid lifestyle inflation — as your income grows, resist the urge to upgrade every aspect of your life at once.
#186High-interest debt should be paid off before you start investing — no investment consistently returns 20%+ risk-free.
#187The debt snowball method: pay off your smallest debt first for quick wins and psychological momentum.
#188The debt avalanche method: pay off the highest-interest debt first to save the most money mathematically.
#189Compound interest works against you in debt just as powerfully as it works for you in savings.
#190Automating savings and debit orders removes the temptation to spend money before it is saved.
#191A sinking fund is money set aside monthly for known future expenses — car services, school fees, annual premiums.
#192The total interest paid on a 20-year home loan often equals 50–80% of the original purchase price.
#193Paying an extra R500 per month into your home loan can reduce a 20-year bond term by three to five years.
#194A child costs an estimated R1–2 million to raise to age 18 in South Africa — plan for this early.
#195Financial stress is the leading cause of relationship breakdown in South Africa — talk openly about money.
#196Teaching children about money through pocket money and budgeting sets them up for lifelong financial success.
#197Budget for irregular expenses — annual premiums, car licences, school registration — they are predictable, not surprises.
#198The rand has lost significant purchasing power over the past 20 years — keeping all savings in cash is risky.
#199Comparison shopping before major purchases can save thousands — especially for electronics and appliances.
#200Financial freedom is not about earning more — it is about spending less than you earn, consistently.
#201Short-term insurance (car, home, contents) is often undervalued — one claim can save years of savings.
#202Comparing short-term insurance quotes annually can save hundreds of rands per month without reducing cover.
#203Travel insurance is often overlooked — one medical emergency abroad can cost more than a year's salary.
#204Buy experiences, not things — research shows experiences provide more lasting happiness than possessions.
#205Starting a side income stream can significantly accelerate your path to financial independence.
#206Frugality is not being cheap — it is about maximising the value you get from every rand you spend.
#207Net worth = assets minus liabilities. Tracking this monthly is one of the clearest measures of financial progress.
#208A financial plan does not need to be complicated — a simple written budget you stick to beats a complex one you ignore.
#209Renegotiating your gym membership, insurance premiums, and internet contract annually is worthwhile — ask for a better rate.
#210Building wealth is more about discipline than income — consistent small actions compound into significant results over time.
Insurance 35 facts
#211Life insurance pays a lump sum to your dependants on your death — it replaces your income-earning ability.
#212Term life insurance provides cover for a fixed period — ideal to cover a bond or until children are independent.
#213Whole life insurance has no end date — premiums are higher but cover is guaranteed for life.
#214Income protection insurance pays a monthly benefit if you are unable to work due to illness or injury.
#215Disability cover pays a lump sum if you are permanently and totally disabled and unable to work.
#216Own-occupation disability cover is more comprehensive than any-occupation cover — the definitions matter greatly.
#217Dread disease (critical illness) cover pays a lump sum on diagnosis of serious conditions like cancer or stroke.
#218Gap cover bridges the shortfall between what your medical aid pays and what specialists actually charge.
#219Life insurance premiums are not tax-deductible in South Africa — but the payout to your beneficiaries is tax-free.
#220Underinsurance is common — most South Africans are covered for far less than their actual income replacement need.
#221A financial needs analysis ensures you are neither over- nor under-insured for your specific circumstances.
#222Waiting periods apply to most insurance policies — pre-existing conditions may be excluded for an initial period.
#223Business assurance protects companies against the financial impact of losing a key person or director.
#224Keyman insurance compensates a business for revenue loss following the death or disability of a critical employee.
#225Many South Africans hold multiple funeral policies — consolidating them can save money and simplify claims.
#226Home loan protection insurance pays off your bond on death — it is sometimes a condition of the home loan.
#227Credit life insurance is often added automatically to loans — cheaper alternatives exist, so shop around.
#228Surrendering a life policy early typically results in significant financial loss — explore all alternatives first.
#229Disability cover typically replaces 75% of your gross income — structure your finances to live on this if needed.
#230Income protection has a waiting period of 1–3 months before it pays out — your emergency fund must bridge this gap.
#231Review your insurance needs after every major life event — marriage, children, home purchase, or job change.
#232Group life cover through your employer disappears when you leave — portable individual policies follow you.
#233Smokers pay significantly higher life and disability premiums — quitting can save thousands of rands per year.
#234Non-disclosure on an insurance application — failing to declare medical history — can lead to claims being rejected.
#235The FSCA (Financial Sector Conduct Authority) regulates all insurance companies operating in South Africa.
#236Complaints about insurance companies can be lodged with the Ombudsman for Long-term or Short-term Insurance.
#237Reviewing your car insurance when your vehicle's value drops significantly can reduce your monthly premium.
#238An insurance excess is the amount you pay before the insurer pays — a higher excess lowers your premium.
#239All-risk cover (contents and portable possessions) protects items you take outside your home.
#240Some insurers offer wellness programme benefits and premium discounts for healthy lifestyle choices.
#241A policy schedule is your legal insurance contract — always read the exclusions section carefully.
#242Cession of a life policy as security for a business loan is common — understand what you are giving up.
#243Disability insurance is often the most overlooked cover — yet you are statistically more likely to be disabled than to die early.
#244Critical illness cover can be structured as an accelerated benefit on a life policy — or as a standalone policy.
#245A nominated beneficiary on a life policy receives the payout directly — it bypasses the estate and probate entirely.
Property 30 facts
#246A home loan in South Africa typically runs over 20 years — the total interest paid often exceeds the purchase price.
#247The prime lending rate is the benchmark for home loan interest in South Africa — it moves with the repo rate.
#248A 1% drop in your home loan rate on a R1.5 million bond saves approximately R15,000 per year in interest.
#249Transfer duty is paid by the buyer — properties below R1,100,000 are currently exempt from transfer duty.
#250Estate agent commission — typically 5–7% plus VAT — is paid by the seller at transfer.
#251Bond registration costs and transfer costs are separate fees — budget for both when buying a property.
#252A pre-approval from a bank tells you your exact buying power before you start house hunting.
#253Sectional title ownership comes with monthly levies and body corporate rules — understand both before buying.
#254Homeowners' association levies in security estates can add significant amounts to your monthly housing cost.
#255Renting out a room or flatlet can substantially subsidise your monthly home loan repayments.
#256Capital Gains Tax applies when you sell a property that is not your primary residence.
#257Your primary residence CGT exclusion is R2 million — gains above this are included in your taxable income at 40%.
#258Property is highly illiquid — it cannot be converted to cash quickly in a financial emergency.
#259The South African property market is highly localised — values differ dramatically between suburbs and provinces.
#260Buying in an emerging area rather than a prime area can deliver superior capital growth over 10+ years.
#261A home inspection before purchase can identify hidden defects that save you from expensive future costs.
#262The voetstoots clause (sold as-is) is common in private property sales — it limits the seller's liability.
#263Sectional title owners must insure the building through the body corporate — but contents must be insured separately.
#264Renovation work rarely returns 100 cents in the rand — improve your home for lifestyle, not as an investment strategy.
#265A buy-to-let property should be evaluated on both rental yield and capital growth — not just one of these.

#267Vacancy periods and maintenance costs reduce the net yield of a rental property significantly — account for both.
#268A reputable rental agent typically charges 8–10% of monthly rent to manage a property on your behalf.
#269Inherited property may attract Capital Gains Tax — seek professional advice before selling.
#270Off-plan properties carry development risk — the completed product may differ from what was sold on plan.
#271A second property is not always the best investment — compare it honestly against a diversified portfolio.
#272Agricultural land investment is a niche requiring specialist knowledge and understanding of planning permission rules.
#273Paying extra into your home loan creates an access bond facility — a powerful emergency fund with no interest cost.
#274The Consumer Protection Act limits the voetstoots clause for sellers who are not private individuals.
#275Property syndications pool investor funds to buy commercial or industrial property — check regulatory compliance carefully.
Medical Aid 25 facts
#276Medical aid is not the same as health insurance — medical aid provides comprehensive in- and out-of-hospital cover.
#277Gap cover only works alongside a medical aid scheme — it is not a stand-alone health product.
#278Hospital plans are entry-level medical aids — they cover hospitalisation but not day-to-day medical expenses.
#279Prescribed minimum benefits (PMBs) are conditions all medical aids must cover in full — regardless of your plan.
#280PMBs include 270 diagnosis-treatment pairs and 26 chronic conditions — you cannot be turned away for these.
#281Open medical aid schemes accept any member regardless of employer — closed schemes are restricted to specific groups.
#282Joining a medical aid young and staying on it locks in more favourable age-based premium structures.
#283Medical savings accounts (MSAs) hold your day-to-day benefits and roll over to the next year if unspent.
#284The medical aid tax credit of R364/month per main member and R246/month per first dependant reduces your tax bill.
#285Waiting periods apply to new medical aid members — typically 3 months general and 12 months for pre-existing conditions.
#286The late joiner penalty increases your premium if you join a medical aid after age 35 without continuous prior cover.
#287Chronic medication under PMBs must be approved by your scheme — ensure your doctor submits the correct paperwork.
#288Specialists in South Africa often charge at 200–400% of medical aid rates — gap cover bridges this shortfall.
#289Always check if a hospital or specialist is on your scheme's designated service provider (DSP) network before booking.
#290Using a non-DSP provider without pre-authorisation can result in significant co-payments or total claim rejection.
#291Pre-authorisation is required for most elective procedures — always get it in writing before you go in.
#292Medical inflation in South Africa consistently runs above general CPI — medical aid increases reflect this reality.
#293Dental and optical benefits are often funded from the savings account on lower plans — budget for these separately.
#294Maternity cover limits are an important consideration for young families — check them before falling pregnant.
#295Many medical aids offer wellness programmes with chronic disease management, gym benefits, and health screenings — use them.
#296Changing medical aids is easiest during the open enrolment period — this minimises waiting periods on your new scheme.
#297If you are unemployed, you can continue your medical aid as a direct payer rather than losing your cover.
#298Medical aid schemes cannot refuse a member based on health status — but they can apply waiting periods and exclusions.
#299The Council for Medical Schemes (CMS) regulates all registered medical aid schemes in South Africa.
#300A financial advisor can help you compare medical aid options objectively — they earn no commission on medical aid advice.
